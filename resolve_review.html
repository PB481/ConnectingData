Bottom Line
Resolve is a competent enterprise data-platform vendor with strong actuarial DNA that has wrapped LLM extraction around a configurable framework. They are not building what you are building, and they are not architecturally equipped to. IntegraLynx is a feeder and overlay system; the Agentic ERP is a core system. Treat them as a benchmark, not a partner.
Section 01

What Resolve Actually Is

Resolve Financial Software is the vendor; IntegraLynx is the platform. They position themselves as "unifying disparate data systems" for fund administrators, custodians, depositaries, insurers, and banks. The team self-describes as actuaries paired with developers — strong domain pedigree, particularly insurance-heavy. Logos shown include Zurich, CNP, Prudential, Mercer, Bank of Ireland, Santander, and Utmost.

The Client Onboarding product is one of seven solutions in their Asset Management line. Its sibling solutions sit directly adjacent to your ERP scope: NAV Oversight (shadow NAV with 150+ controls per fund), Fund Data Management, Annex IV / Form PF, Cost Disclosures, CCI, and AI in Asset Management.

Section 02

What the Client Onboarding Solution Does

Stripping the marketing copy, the substance is an LLM-driven document extraction and orchestration layer for fund onboarding. It ingests IMAs, prospectuses, supplements, fee letters, and side letters; extracts structured fields (parties, share classes, fee rules, hurdles, dates, counterparties); routes low-confidence cases to human reviewers via a stoplight workflow; then pushes validated data into downstream fund accounting, GL, client master, sub-ledger, and reporting systems via APIs, flat files, or connectors.

A genuinely interesting adjunct: they pitch pre-engagement extraction — running publicly available prospectuses through the engine before contracts are signed, to produce a "data snapshot" that informs sales scoping and pricing. That overlaps conceptually with your effort-based pricing engine, just from the document side rather than the operational complexity side.

The headline claim — onboarding cycles compressed from 12–18 months to "weeks" — is the standard fund-admin transition pain point, expressed in the standard way.

Section 03

Honest Read on the Marketing Claims

The page is carefully worded to imply more than it commits to. Phrases such as "designed to comply with GDPR," "built with the expectation of SOC-type compliance (you can layer your own controls)," and "reference knowledge models evolve from usage" are aspirational framings rather than concrete claims.

⚠ Yellow Flag
The single client testimonial on the Client Onboarding page ("Head of Technology, Dublin-based life assurance client") appears reused on the NAV Oversight page with slightly different attribution ("Head of Technology, Global Financial Services Client"). Same quote, different page. This raises a question about the maturity of the asset management book of business versus the insurance book.

There is no architecture detail, no model evaluation methodology, no description of extraction confidence calibration, no mention of formal verification or deterministic validation of extracted data. The "LLM Engine" sits in their platform diagram as one block alongside "Calculations" and "Rules" — i.e. it is framed as one capability among many, not as the architectural centre of gravity.

Section 05

Can They Help Build the ERP?

Three honest answers depending on what is meant by "help."

No
As a vendor whose platform you'd build on top of
IntegraLynx is positioned as the unification fabric for existing back-office systems. Building an agentic ERP on top of someone else's platform inverts your architectural thesis — you become the application, they become the data layer, and your differentiator is subordinated to their orchestration model. The moat is lost.
Probably Not
As a build / co-development partner
Their pitch is "actuaries + developers configuring our framework." That is a configuration-and-implementation engagement model, not a co-development model for novel architecture. Your build needs people who can reason about Z3 SMT proofs, design neuro-symbolic calculation pipelines, and build property-based test harnesses for accounting code generation. Their public materials don't show that capability.
Maybe
As a tactical partner for one specific module
The onboarding document-to-structured-data extraction problem is real and well-defined. Licensing their extraction as an upstream feeder to the Reader stage could be a build-vs-buy conversation worth having — provided contracts leave you free to swap them out, since extraction schemas would shape your downstream pipeline. Their pre-engagement extraction overlaps interestingly with the pricing engine.
Section 06

Strategic Risks if You Engage Them

Risk 01
Disclosure asymmetry
Discovery conversations require you to describe your architecture; they describe their roadmap in marketing terms. Their AI in Asset Management page tells you what they are planning to build that overlaps with what you are already building.
Risk 02
Positioning leak
If they are moving toward agentic territory (their AI in Asset Management solution suggests they are), giving them visibility into your dual-builder, Z3, and Tool Library architecture hands them a roadmap.
Risk 03
"Partner" capture
Implementation partners often try to convert pilots into framework lock-in. IntegraLynx becomes the integration hub; your ERP becomes one module inside their platform diagram.
Risk 04
Verification philosophy mismatch
Their model is human-in-the-loop on exceptions; yours is mathematical proof on every calculation. These are different epistemic stances. Bolting your verification onto their orchestration creates a fragile boundary.
Section 07

Recommended Posture

  1. Treat them as a benchmark, not a partner. Use their public materials to calibrate your positioning. What they don't claim — formal verification, deterministic guarantees, code generation rather than data extraction — is the gap your messaging should occupy. Their "Trust is key" testimonial is essentially the same value proposition you make, just without the architectural backing. Your story is why the trust is justified.
  2. Take a short discovery call only with a specific, narrow question — for example, "what is your extraction accuracy on Irish ICAV prospectuses across share class fee structures?" Go in with a non-disclosure stance about your own architecture.
  3. Watch their AI in Asset Management page. That is where their roadmap will leak. If they begin claiming code generation, deterministic verification, or formal methods, the competitive picture changes and warrants a fresh review.
  4. Reuse the pre-engagement extraction idea, on your own terms. Their concept of running public fund documents through extraction before contracts are signed is genuinely useful. It is also straightforward to build inside the Reader stage of your pipeline and feeds directly into the effort-based pricing engine. Build, do not buy.
Closing

Summary

IntegraLynx Client Onboarding occupies a real and valuable slot in the workflow — the unstructured-document-to-structured-data conversion at fund take-on — that your platform also has to solve. Their NAV Oversight product is the closer competitor philosophically (shadow calcs, break detection, audit trail), but it remains an oversight layer rather than the calculation engine itself.

They are competent at what they do. They are not the right partner for what you are doing.

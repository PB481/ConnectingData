    </div>
    <h1>How This Fund Works</h1>
    <p class="subtitle">A bespoke single-investor structure. This sheet explains the moving parts in everyday language so the team can review and prepare their own notes.</p>
  </header>

  <div class="nutshell">
    <span class="lbl">In one sentence</span>
    <p>An Australian Unit Trust puts money into an Irish company (the DAC). The DAC buys European loans. The income from those loans flows back to the investor two ways — a <strong>quarterly coupon</strong> (cash) and a <strong>daily change in value</strong> (the NAV). Our job is to value it daily and make sure the money moving in and out is correct.</p>
  </div>

  <!-- THE STACK -->
  <section>
    <div class="sec-head">
      <span class="sec-num">01</span>
      <h2>The Picture — a stack of three layers</h2>
      <span class="rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="stack">
      <div class="layer investor">
        <span class="tag">Layer 1 · The Investor</span>
        <div class="name">Australian Unit Trust</div>
        <div class="desc">One single investor. Wants to see a value every day, and can put money in or take it out once a month.</div>
        <div class="pill-row">
          <span class="pill">Daily NAV</span>
          <span class="pill">Monthly liquidity</span>
          <span class="pill">Sole investor</span>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="connector">
        <div class="spine">
          <span class="arrowhead top">▲</span>
          <span class="arrowhead bot">▼</span>
        </div>
        <span class="label-up">value &amp; coupon flow up</span>
        <span class="label-down">cash for investing flows down</span>
      </div>

      <div class="layer dac">
        <span class="tag">Layer 2 · The Irish SPV</span>
        <div class="name">The DAC (Section 110 company)</div>
        <div class="desc">The company in the middle. The investor's money sits here as a <strong>Profit Participation Note (PPN)</strong> — think of the PPN as the pipe that carries all the DAC's profit up to the investor.</div>
        <div class="pill-row">
          <span class="pill">PPN = the investor's holding</span>
          <span class="pill">Pays coupon quarterly</span>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="connector">
        <div class="spine">
          <span class="arrowhead top">▲</span>
          <span class="arrowhead bot">▼</span>
        </div>
        <span class="label-up">interest &amp; value</span>
        <span class="label-down">money to buy loans</span>
      </div>

      <div class="layer assets">
        <span class="tag">Layer 3 · The Assets</span>
        <div class="name">European Syndicated Loans</div>
        <div class="desc">What the DAC actually owns. They earn interest and move up and down in value. These prices are what drive the daily NAV.</div>
        <div class="pill-row">
          <span class="pill">Not exchange-traded</span>
          <span class="pill">Slow to settle</span>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <p class="flowkey">Money &amp; value flow <strong>up</strong> the stack (loans → DAC → investor). Cash for investing flows <strong>down</strong> (investor → DAC → loans).</p>
  </section>

  <!-- TWO RETURNS -->
  <section>
    <div class="sec-head">
      <span class="sec-num">02</span>
      <h2>The two things the investor gets back</h2>
      <span class="rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="two-col">
      <div class="card">
        <span class="ctag">Return A</span>
        <h3>Daily value — the NAV</h3>
        <p>Every day we value the loans, add the income earned so far, take off the DAC's costs. What's left is the value of the PPN — and that's the investor's NAV. It changes daily because loan prices and accrued income change daily.</p>
        <span class="freq">EVERY DAY</span>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <span class="ctag">Return B</span>
        <h3>Quarterly coupon — cash</h3>
        <p>Four times a year, the profit built up inside the DAC is paid out to the investor as cash. The coupon is <strong>not extra</strong> — it's profit that was already sitting in the daily NAV, now being paid out.</p>
        <span class="freq">EVERY QUARTER</span>
      </div>
      <p class="same-money">It's the same money — it just moves from “value” to “cash” on payment day. When the coupon is paid, the NAV steps down by that amount.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- NAV FOUR LINES -->
  <section>
    <div class="sec-head">
      <span class="sec-num">03</span>
      <h2>How the daily NAV is built — just four lines</h2>
      <span class="rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="nav-calc">
      <div class="nav-row">
        <span class="nav-op"></span>
        <span class="nav-txt">Value of the loans <em>(today's prices)</em></span>
      </div>
      <div class="nav-row">
        <span class="nav-op">+</span>
        <span class="nav-txt">Income earned but not yet paid <em>(accrued interest)</em></span>
      </div>
      <div class="nav-row">
        <span class="nav-op">−</span>
        <span class="nav-txt">The DAC's costs and the small margin it keeps</span>
      </div>
      <div class="nav-row total">
        <span class="nav-op">=</span>
        <span class="nav-txt">Value of the PPN = the investor's NAV</span>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="flowkey" style="text-align:left; max-width:640px; margin-top:14px;">If you can produce these four lines from the standard reports, you understand the engine. The <strong>coupon</strong> comes from the same numbers — loan income, less costs, less the retained margin. <strong>We should be able to calculate it ourselves; the Manager confirms it, they don't hand us a number we can't reproduce.</strong></p>
  </section>

  <!-- WHO DOES WHAT -->
  <section>
    <div class="sec-head">
      <span class="sec-num">04</span>
      <h2>Who does what</h2>
      <span class="rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="roles">
      <div class="role us">
        <div class="who">Us · Fund Accounting</div>
        <div class="what">Value the loans daily, produce the NAV, calculate the coupon, check the cash.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="role mgr">
        <div class="who">The Manager · PE Firm</div>
        <div class="what">Decides which loans to buy/sell, instructs cash movements, confirms the coupon.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="role maples">
        <div class="who">Maples · Corporate Services &amp; Trustee</div>
        <div class="what">Keeps the Irish company alive and legal — company secretary, directors, filings — and acts as trustee. Does <strong>not</strong> do the daily accounting; that's us.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="role depo">
        <div class="who">No Depositary</div>
        <div class="what">There's no extra independent watchdog above us. That means <strong>our checks are the main safeguard</strong> — which raises the importance of what the team does.</div>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- WATCH LIST -->
  <section>
    <div class="sec-head">
      <span class="sec-num">05</span>
      <h2>Three things to watch</h2>
      <span class="rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="watch">
      <div class="watch-item">
        <div class="wn">01</div>
        <h4>Loan pricing</h4>
        <p>These loans don't trade on an exchange, so a reliable daily price is the hardest part of the NAV. Stale or missing prices need a documented process — not a guess.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="watch-item">
        <div class="wn">02</div>
        <h4>The coupon hand-off</h4>
        <p>Make sure profit paid out as the coupon doesn't <em>also</em> stay sitting in the NAV (no double-counting), and that the NAV steps down correctly on payment day.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="watch-item">
        <div class="wn">03</div>
        <h4>Cash timing</h4>
        <p>The investor can redeem monthly, but loans take weeks to sell. Cash out may be needed before cash comes in. Every Manager cash instruction is checked against the NAV and actual cash before release.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- CAVEAT -->
  <div class="caveat">
    <span class="lbl">The one caveat — keep this straight</span>
    <p>It behaves <strong>like</strong> a feeder fund into a master fund — the Unit Trust is the feeder, the DAC is the master, the loans are the master's portfolio. That's the right mental model for how it runs day to day. <strong>But legally the DAC is a debt-issuing company and the investor holds a note, not fund units.</strong> So we say it <em>operates like</em> a master-feeder — we don't call it one in the legal or regulatory documents.</p>
  </div>

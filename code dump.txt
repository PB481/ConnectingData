    <h2>The useful mental model</h2>
    <p>Think of the DAC as a fund-of-one with a single notional share class. The investor (AUT) is the only holder. The PPN face value is the &ldquo;units outstanding.&rdquo; The portfolio of syndicated loans is the &ldquo;underlying holdings.&rdquo; The quarterly coupon is the &ldquo;distribution.&rdquo; This analogy unlocks <strong>roughly 70% of what your FA team already knows</strong> &mdash; daily NAV mechanics, pricing, accruals, audit support. Use it to onboard the team. But know where it breaks before you build the operating model.</p>

  <div class="guardrails">
    <h2>How to use this analogy safely</h2>
    <ol>
      <li><strong>Use the left column for onboarding.</strong> When introducing the structure to the FA team, lean into the fund mental model. It accelerates learning and uses skills they already have.</li>
      <li><strong>Use the right column for operating model design.</strong> When designing workflows, controls, and tooling, work from what the DAC actually is &mdash; a Section 110 company with a PPN &mdash; not from fund analogies.</li>
      <li><strong>Flag the right-column items in every training session.</strong> The fund analogy carries dangerous unstated assumptions. Make them explicit so they don&rsquo;t silently propagate into procedures.</li>
      <li><strong>Build documentation around the right column.</strong> Scope of services, risk register, and procedures should be written from the DAC&rsquo;s actual mechanics &mdash; not the fund analogy.</li>
      <li><strong>Use the fund language carefully with external parties.</strong> Tax adviser, auditor, ManCo, and Trustee will all be confused if you describe the PPN as &ldquo;a share class.&rdquo; Internally fine; externally use the formal terminology.</li>
    </ol>
